# EDU498 Class 7 Layered Genius Mini-Unit

**Designing a Minecraft World That Carries Identity and Community Knowledge**

## Student

**Ethan - Computer Science**

Ethan comes from a Seneca family and is proud of his Haudenosaunee heritage. He is also 25% white and sometimes feels unsure about how to represent both parts of himself. He enjoys computer science, coding, robotics, game design, problem-solving, building simple games, learning programming languages, and exploring emerging technologies such as artificial intelligence. He is especially interested in how digital tools can support community engagement, storytelling, and the preservation of cultural knowledge. Outside of school, he enjoys the outdoors and learning about the history, traditions, and contemporary life of the Seneca Nation.

**Identity markers:** Indigenous (Seneca), aspiring computer scientist, technology-oriented, analytical, collaborative, community-minded, culturally connected, and creative problem-solver.

## Brainstorm Area

### Identity

Students design a Minecraft place that can represent identity, community, memory, belonging, or future possibility. For Ethan, this creates space to connect coding and game design with Seneca / Haudenosaunee heritage, mixed identity, outdoor connection, community storytelling, and cultural knowledge without forcing private disclosure.

### Joy

Joy comes through choice, building, testing, debugging, peer support, visible progress, and sharing a world students created. Minecraft makes joy part of the learning structure because students can see their ideas become playable and meaningful.

### Skills

Students practice sequencing, decomposition, debugging, spatial reasoning, MakeCode / Minecraft Education tool use, planning before building, screenshot use, partner communication, and oral or written explanation.

### Intellect

Students build understanding that code and design communicate meaning. They think about how paths, signs, structures, colors, repeated patterns, and interactions guide an audience through a digital world and tell a story about identity or community.

### Criticality

Students begin asking whose stories are represented in digital worlds, whose knowledge is missing, how technology can preserve or erase community stories, and what respectful use means when designing with cultural symbols, community knowledge, or shared histories.
